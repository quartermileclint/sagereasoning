6 layers of agents

Key structural recommendations from the video for building AI agents (based on the emerging 6-layer infrastructure stack):
The video argues that most people are building agents on fragile, transitional layers that will evolve or disappear in the next 12–18 months (similar to early cloud shifts). To future-proof your agents, you must intentionally design them with stack literacy — understanding and architecting around these six maturing layers rather than treating tools as simple “Lego bricks.” Here’s exactly what the video says you should structurally build in:
•  Layer 1: Compute & Sandboxing Build agents to run in isolated, secure sandboxes (never on laptops or unsupervised environments). Choose your architectural bet early: ephemeral (disposable microVMs like E2B — fast, stateless, great for one-off tasks) vs. persistent (long-lived Docker-style like Daytona — for agents that need ongoing state). This layer is already relatively mature; design for it now so your agents scale reliably without rework.
•  Layer 2: Identity & Communication Give agents a verifiable, native internet presence instead of relying on transitional “shims” like email (e.g., Agent Mail). Email works today but creates rate limits, spam issues, and future migration pain. Structurally embed support for emerging agent-native protocols (on-chain identity, Ato-style comms, or MCP). Avoid long-term lock-in to human-centric hacks.
•  Layer 3: Memory & Statefulness Build cross-session, portable memory into every agent (not just in-context prompts). Use or design around systems like Mem0 (hybrid graph/vector/key-value with active curation) that let users own their data. Prioritize portability and user-owned memory over platform-specific storage — hyperscalers may absorb this layer, so avoid designs that create lock-in.
•  Layer 4: Tools & Integration Architect agents with middleware for auth, connectors, observability, and rate-limit handling (e.g., patterns from Compose). The N×M integration problem (every agent to every tool) is real and painful. Build for standardization where possible (MCP could help) but expect enterprises to move slowly — make integrations sustainable and observable, not brittle one-off hacks.
•  Layer 5: Provisioning & Billing Design agents to securely discover, provision, and pay for services on-the-fly (agent-legible CLI-style commands, tokenized payments via Stripe Projects, metered/dynamic budgets). Structurally include agent-to-agent payment flows and scaling-to-zero logic so agents can operate autonomously without manual oversight.
•  Layer 6: Orchestration & Coordination (the currently missing layer) This is the biggest gap and the highest-leverage place to build. For multi-agent systems, embed lifecycle management, scheduling, supervision, failure recovery, merging outputs, hierarchies, and FinOps. Think “Kubernetes for agents.” Hand-rolling it today is common, but the video says the next big opportunity (and risk of agent sprawl) lives here — design your agents assuming a coordination layer will exist soon.
Three overarching builder truisms the video says you must structurally enforce (not optional):
•  Reliability compounds negatively (five 99% layers = ~95% system reliability) → design for compounding failure prevention.
•  Avoid transitional lock-in (shims, platform-specific memory, etc.) → always prioritize portability and open standards.
•  Prevent agent sprawl (like microservices gone wrong) → build with orchestration and governance from day one.
Bottom line from the video:
Stop building agents as if the current tools are permanent. Instead, structurally engineer them with explicit awareness of these six layers, choose durable bets over shiny shims, and treat stack literacy as non-negotiable. Teams who do this now will avoid the reliability failures and migration headaches hitting fast-moving builders in 2026.
This gives your agents a solid, evolvable foundation rather than fragile composability on top of mismatched parts.
