# The Passion Taxonomy

## What Are Passions?

Passions are not emotions. They are **cognitive errors**—false judgements about what is good or evil. When you misjudge what's truly valuable, your reasoning derails, and passionate distortions follow. Understanding the passion taxonomy enables you to recognize and examine these specific false judgements.

## The 2x2 Matrix

Passions organize along two axes:

**Temporal dimension:** Present or Future
**Evaluative dimension:** Apparent Good or Apparent Evil

This creates four root passions:

### 1. Epithumia (Craving)
**Future Apparent Good** — Intense desire for something you believe will benefit you but cannot control.

Sub-species:
- **Anger** — Craving for punishment or retaliation
- **Longing** — Craving for an absent person or thing
- **Love of Pleasure** — Craving for sensory gratification
- **Love of Wealth** — Craving for material accumulation
- **Love of Honour** — Craving for recognition and status

### 2. Hedone (Irrational Pleasure)
**Present Apparent Good** — False elation from believing something present is genuinely valuable when it is not.

Sub-species:
- **Enchantment** — Intoxicated delight in what appears good
- **Malicious Joy** — Delight in another's misfortune

### 3. Phobos (Fear)
**Future Apparent Evil** — Dread about future circumstances you believe are genuinely bad but cannot control.

Sub-species:
- **Terror** — Intense, often paralyzing fear
- **Timidity** — Habitual fearfulness, reluctance to act
- **Shame** — Fear of social judgment or loss of reputation

### 4. Lupe (Distress)
**Present Apparent Evil** — Anguish from believing something present is genuinely bad and cannot be changed.

Sub-species:
- **Envy** — Distress at another's good fortune
- **Pity** — Distress at another's hardship
- **Grief** — Distress over loss or absence

## The Three Rational Good Feelings (Eupatheiai)

Virtue is not passionless. When you reason rightly, three rational affections naturally arise:

- **Boulesis** — Rational wish; desire grounded in what's truly good (virtue)
- **Eulabeia** — Rational caution; prudent care without irrational fear
- **Chara** — Rational joy; genuine delight in virtue and right understanding

## The Diagnostic, Not Punitive, Approach

The passion taxonomy serves one purpose: **to name the specific false judgement so you can examine it.**

When SageReasoning identifies that epithumia (craving for wealth) distorted your reasoning, it's not condemning you. It's pointing to the exact moment where you confused something outside your control with something genuinely good. Once named, you can inspect that judgement. Is it true? Can you actually control that outcome? What is genuinely good in this situation?

This diagnostic approach enables growth. You cannot correct a false judgement you haven't recognized. The taxonomy makes recognition possible.
