# How Scoring Works

SageReasoning evaluates reasoning through a 4-stage pipeline that moves from foundational moral clarity through to alignment with virtue. Rather than producing a single numeric score, the system generates qualitative proximity levels and diagnostic feedback.

## The Four Evaluation Stages

### Stage 1: Control Filter (Prohairesis)
The first question: What lies within your moral choice, and what doesn't? The control filter separates what you can directly control (your judgements, impulses, and will) from what you cannot (external outcomes, others' opinions, circumstance). This stage establishes the boundary of moral responsibility.

### Stage 2: Appropriate Action Assessment (Kathekon)
Given what's within your control, is this action reasonable? This stage evaluates whether the proposed choice represents a fitting response to the situation—regardless of external outcome. A kathekon action aligns with reason and nature, even if circumstances defeat it.

### Stage 3: Passion Diagnosis
Which false judgements distorted your reasoning? Rather than labeling you as flawed, this stage identifies the specific passions (cognitive errors) at work: Did you crave something you cannot control? Judge present circumstances as intrinsically evil? Lose sight of what's actually good? This diagnostic approach enables self-examination and growth.

### Stage 4: Unified Virtue Assessment
How close is your reasoning to the sage ideal? This final stage synthesizes all prior analysis into a **qualitative proximity level**:

- **Reflexive** — Unreasoned reaction, automatic impulse
- **Habitual** — Practiced behavior without active reflection
- **Deliberate** — Conscious reasoning applied to the choice
- **Principled** — Reasoning grounded in explicit philosophical commitment
- **Sage-like** — Perfect alignment with virtue and rational nature

## Why Qualitative Over Numeric

Numeric scores suggest false precision. Stoic reasoning is inherently qualitative—it's about the *nature* of your thought, not its measurable distance from some abstract ideal. The proximity levels describe how your reasoning relates to wisdom.
